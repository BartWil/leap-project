The Problem

Why LEAP matters

Lower-extremity apophyseal pathologies — Osgood–Schlatter disease and Sever disease — are among the most prevalent musculoskeletal conditions in youth athletes, yet they remain poorly understood and undertreated.

Current clinical practice frequently defaults to a "wait and see" approach, despite growing evidence that these conditions are not trivially self-limiting. Recent data show that adults with a history of Osgood–Schlatter disease have significantly worse knee health and a higher risk of developing patellar tendinopathy (Krommes et al., 2025). Meanwhile, 25% of practitioners still recommend watchful waiting as the primary strategy (Lyng et al., 2020).

No high-quality randomized controlled trial of conservative treatment currently exists for this population. The LEAP project aims to close that gap.

Our Approach

A multidomain framework

Biomarker Panel

Cartilage turnover, bone turnover, and inflammatory markers (CRP, vitamin D, IL-family) to characterize the biological landscape of apophyseal pathology.

Ultrasound Imaging

Structural assessment of the apophysis at baseline and post-intervention to track morphological changes alongside clinical outcomes.

Functional Testing

Validated performance-based and patient-reported outcomes (KOOS-Child, OxAFQ-C, NPRS) alongside biomechanical assessment.

Controlled Trials

Rigorous randomized and prospective designs — from a double-blind sham-controlled LLLT pilot RCT to an individualized rehabilitation feasibility study.

Registered Studies

Two linked clinical studies

LEAP-PHEN and LEAP-REHAB are methodologically linked — participants completing the photobiomodulation trial may continue into the rehabilitation study through a post-trial pathway.

LEAP-PHEN

Multidomain Phenotyping of Lower-Extremity Apophyseal Pain in Youth Athletes Using Biomarkers, Ultrasound, and Functional Testing, with an Embedded Double-Blind Pilot Photobiomodulation Trial

● Recruiting NCT07446517 ↗

Work Package 1 — Cross-Sectional Phenotyping

Comparison of biomarker profiles, ultrasound findings, and functional parameters between youth athletes with LEAP (OSD + Sever) and a healthy matched control group. Each participant provides 5 ml of venous blood for analysis of CBC, CRP, vitamin D, and bone and cartilage turnover markers.

Work Package 2 — Pilot RCT (LLLT vs. Sham)

The first randomized, double-blind, sham-controlled trial of low-level laser therapy (photobiomodulation) in youth athletes with apophyseal pain. Active LLLT uses a class 3B GaAlAs laser applied to the symptomatic apophyseal region over 10 sessions / 2 weeks.

  • Masking: Triple — participant, investigator, outcomes assessor
  • Allocation: 1:1 randomization (active LLLT vs. sham)
  • Primary objective: Feasibility (recruitment, retention, adherence, safety)
  • Secondary: Between-group differences in pain (NPRS) and function
  • Exploratory: Biomarker and ultrasound changes to design a future full-scale RCT

Scientific Rationale for LLLT

A 2022 meta-analysis demonstrated significant reductions in pain and dysfunction with LLLT in tendinopathy and plantar fasciitis (Naterstad et al.). LLLT reduces pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-1, IL-6, IL-8, IL-18) and mediators (PGE2, TNF-α, IL-6), with mechanism overlap relevant to apophyseal pathology (Berni et al., 2023). No prior evidence exists for LLLT in LEAP.

LEAP-REHAB

Feasibility, Clinical Response, and Exploratory Predictors of an Individualized Load-Based Rehabilitation Pathway in Youth Athletes With Osgood-Schlatter Disease or Sever Disease: A Prospective Single-Arm Interventional Study

◌ Not yet recruiting NCT07521280 ↗

Rehabilitation Pathway

A 12-week individualized rehabilitation program built on a standardized core framework, individualized by symptom response rather than fixed timelines. The program targets load tolerance and movement quality in tasks relevant to youth sport.

  • Education and pain monitoring (activity diary)
  • Activity-ladder approach with symptom-guided progression
  • Motor-control training and basic strength exposure
  • Squatting, single-leg control, step/split-squat, calf loading
  • Gradual return to running, jumping, deceleration, sport-specific tasks

Entry Pathways

Participants may enter through three routes (used for descriptive subgroup analyses, not treatment arms):

  • Post-trial: after completing LEAP-PHEN (NCT07446517)
  • Direct entry — active symptoms: current OSD or Sever pain
  • Direct entry — residual: previous symptoms, low-irritability presentation

Primary Objectives (Feasibility)

  • Attendance at planned in-person physiotherapy review visits
  • Home-exercise adherence ≥70% of study weeks
  • Intervention fidelity (protocol elements delivered and documented)
  • Retention at follow-up assessment
  • Safety — adverse events count and classification

Secondary Outcomes

  • Pain: NPRS worst pain in last 7 days (baseline → 12 weeks)
  • Knee function: KOOS-Child (OSD subgroup)
  • Foot/ankle function: OxAFQ-C (Sever subgroup)
  • Return-to-sport status (ordinal participation categories)
  • Patient Global Impression of Change (PGIC, ≥"much improved")

Open Data

De-identified individual participant data, statistical analysis plan, and analytic code will be made openly available through OSF after primary publication, for at least 5 years.
