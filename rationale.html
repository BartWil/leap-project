01

Research Gap

Prevalence & burden

Lower-extremity apophyseal pathologies (LEAP) — principally Osgood–Schlatter disease (OSD), Sever disease (calcaneal apophysitis), and Sinding–Larsen–Johansson syndrome (SLJ) — are among the most prevalent musculoskeletal conditions in skeletally immature athletes. OSD affects an estimated 10–20% of adolescents engaged in sport, with higher rates in sports involving repetitive jumping, running, and kicking. Sever disease accounts for up to 8% of all sports-related injuries presenting to pediatric sports medicine clinics.

Despite this prevalence, the biological, biomechanical, and contextual factors that determine who develops symptoms, who recovers, and who experiences persistent or recurrent pain remain poorly characterised. Clinical presentations vary substantially — from self-limiting episodes to prolonged, functionally disabling courses — yet no validated staging system, prognostic biomarker, or clinically actionable predictor of outcome currently exists.

Current clinical practice

Surveys of clinicians managing LEAP reveal a fragmented and inconsistent practice landscape. Approximately 25% of practitioners continue to recommend a watchful waiting approach as the primary management strategy (Lyng et al., 2020). Activity restriction — including complete cessation of sport — remains common, despite the lack of evidence that it accelerates recovery and the well-documented psychosocial consequences of sport withdrawal in adolescents.

Where active treatment is pursued, available options target symptoms rather than underlying pathology: patient education, pain monitoring, anti-inflammatory medications, stretching and strengthening exercises, ice application, and orthotics. The selection among these options is largely empirical, not evidence-based. If conservative management fails, surgical intervention is occasionally recommended — an approach that is rare, high-risk relative to the condition's natural history, and unsupported by high-quality trials.

The "wait and see" problem

Many young patients are told to rest and wait for growth plates to close. This approach is frustrating for athletes and families, offers no functional support, and may delay the development of adequate load-tolerance — leaving athletes vulnerable to re-injury on return to sport.

The evidence vacuum

A critical review of the literature reveals a striking absence of high-quality interventional evidence. No randomized controlled trial of any conservative treatment currently exists for LEAP in a youth athlete population. The available studies are characterised by small samples, non-randomized designs, heterogeneous case definitions, short follow-up periods, and a near-exclusive focus on symptomatic outcomes without any mechanistic or biomarker data.

This evidence vacuum has direct clinical consequences. Without comparative effectiveness data, clinicians cannot make evidence-based recommendations. Without mechanistic data, it is impossible to know whether treatments that reduce pain do so by addressing the underlying tissue pathology or merely by masking symptoms. Without validated outcome measures adapted to this population, measurement in both research and clinical practice remains inconsistent.

Long-term consequences

The assumption that LEAP is a benign, self-limiting condition has been directly challenged by recent longitudinal data. Krommes et al. (2025) demonstrated that adults with a history of OSD exhibit significantly worse knee health scores and a substantially higher risk of developing patellar tendinopathy compared to matched controls without OSD history. This finding reframes the clinical significance of the condition: inadequate management during the symptomatic phase may have lasting consequences for musculoskeletal health into adulthood.

Additionally, qualitative data indicate that many young athletes and their families experience prolonged uncertainty, frustration, and disengagement from healthcare when advised simply to wait. The social and psychological burden of sport withdrawal — for adolescents whose identity, social networks, and mental health are closely tied to sport participation — is rarely acknowledged in clinical encounters or measured in studies.

02

Study Rationale

Why a multidomain design?

Single-domain studies — focusing solely on pain, or only on imaging, or only on a clinical intervention — cannot capture the multifactorial nature of LEAP. Pain is an imperfect proxy for tissue state. Imaging changes do not always correspond to symptom severity. Function and participation outcomes matter independently of biological measures.

The LEAP-PHEN design integrates three parallel data streams — biomarkers, ultrasound, and functional/patient-reported outcomes — measured at the same time points in the same participants. This allows cross-domain associations to be examined prospectively and provides a richer characterisation of the condition than any single-domain approach. It also generates the measurement infrastructure (validated tools, reference ranges, baseline data) needed to power and design a future full-scale RCT.

Pilot logic

Both LEAP-PHEN and LEAP-REHAB are explicitly designed as feasibility and pilot studies. Their primary outputs are not definitive treatment effects but rather the data and operational evidence needed to justify, power, and correctly design future confirmatory trials. This is methodologically appropriate given the complete absence of prior controlled data.

Biomarker rationale

Bone and cartilage biomarkers offer a window into tissue-level processes that are invisible to clinical examination and ultrasound. In the context of apophyseal pathology, relevant biological processes include:

  • Cartilage turnover — markers such as COMP (cartilage oligomeric matrix protein) and CTX-II may reflect apophyseal cartilage stress and degradation during periods of high mechanical loading coinciding with growth.
  • Bone turnover — markers including P1NP, osteocalcin, and CTX-I may capture the balance between bone formation and resorption at the apophysis, which is mechanically driven and growth-stage dependent.
  • Inflammatory markers — CRP, IL-6, and related cytokines are relevant given the proposed inflammatory component of apophyseal pathology and the known anti-inflammatory effects of candidate treatments including LLLT.
  • Vitamin D — deficiency is associated with musculoskeletal pain and impaired bone metabolism in adolescents; its role as a covariate and potential modifier in LEAP has not been examined.

No prior study has characterised this biomarker profile in youth athletes with LEAP or compared it to healthy matched controls. The cross-sectional WP1 data from LEAP-PHEN will provide the first such characterisation, and the longitudinal WP2 data will allow exploratory assessment of whether LLLT-induced changes in biomarker profiles track with clinical and functional outcomes.

Rationale for low-level laser therapy (LLLT)

Photobiomodulation (low-level laser therapy; LLLT) exerts its effects through photochemical rather than thermal mechanisms. Photons absorbed by mitochondrial chromophores — principally cytochrome c oxidase — enhance electron transport chain activity, increase ATP production, modulate reactive oxygen species, and activate downstream signalling cascades that influence inflammation, cell proliferation, and tissue repair.

The mechanistic rationale for LLLT in LEAP is supported by several converging lines of evidence:

  • A 2022 meta-analysis (Naterstad et al.) demonstrated that LLLT significantly reduces pain and dysfunction in tendinopathy and plantar fasciitis — conditions sharing anatomical proximity and some pathophysiological features with apophyseal pathology.
  • LLLT consistently reduces expression of pro-inflammatory cytokines including IL-1β, IL-6, IL-8, and IL-18, and downregulates PGE2 and TNF-α (Berni et al., 2023) — markers that are implicated in LEAP pathophysiology.
  • LLLT has demonstrated effects on bone metabolism in animal and clinical models, including enhanced osteoblast activity and modulation of bone turnover markers — directly relevant to the apophyseal context.
  • LLLT is non-invasive, painless, and has an established safety profile in paediatric populations.

Critically, no study has examined LLLT in youth athletes with apophyseal pain. The LEAP-PHEN WP2 pilot RCT is therefore the first controlled investigation of this intervention in this population, using a triple-masked, sham-controlled design with standardised device parameters and a mechanistically motivated biomarker outcome battery.

Rationale for individualized load-based rehabilitation

Current rehabilitation approaches for LEAP are largely empirical, rest-focused, and symptom-suppressive. An activity-ladder, symptom-guided rehabilitation model — grounded in load management principles from tendinopathy rehabilitation literature — offers a conceptually coherent alternative that targets load tolerance and graduated return to sport rather than symptom avoidance.

The core principles of the LEAP-REHAB pathway are:

  • Pain monitoring as a guide, not a barrier — participants use the NPRS to monitor pain during and after exercise, adjusting load rather than stopping activity entirely.
  • Progressive loading — exercise dose is progressed according to symptom response and movement quality, not by fixed timelines, allowing individualisation within a standardised framework.
  • Motor-control and strength emphasis — addressing the functional deficits (quadriceps weakness, altered movement patterns, reduced single-leg control) that may both contribute to and result from apophyseal pain.
  • Structured return to sport — the activity-ladder approach provides a systematic framework for reintegrating sport-specific loading, reducing the risk of abrupt load spikes on return.

As a feasibility study, LEAP-REHAB will provide the first prospective data on the feasibility of delivering this approach in Polish youth athletes, and exploratory predictor analyses will identify baseline features that may moderate response — informing subgroup targeting in a future RCT.

03

Conceptual Framework

The LEAP program is grounded in an apophyseal-specific conceptual framework developed through a registered scoping review (OSF: A9ECQ). The framework adapts established sports injury models to the developmental biology and mechanical context unique to the growing skeleton.

Mechanobiological load–capacity model

At the core of the framework is a mechanobiological logic: apophyseal injury occurs when mechanical demand exceeds the load-bearing capacity of apophyseal tissue. This is not a simple threshold model — capacity is not fixed, but dynamically regulated by growth stage, training history, recovery, nutrition, and systemic factors.

The apophyseal cartilage — particularly the traction apophysis under the pull of major muscle-tendon units — is mechanically distinct from articular cartilage. It is subject to tensile rather than compressive loading, has a higher cell density and metabolic rate during active growth, and is directly coupled to the bone-tendon junction in a region that undergoes rapid structural change during the pubertal growth spurt.

Mechanical Demand > Tissue Capacity → Apophyseal pathology

Capacity is modulated by: growth stage · maturation · training load history · recovery · systemic factors (vitamin D, nutrition, sleep)

Time-limited vulnerability window

A central construct in the LEAP framework is the concept of a developmental vulnerability window — a period during which apophyseal tissue is transiently at heightened risk of overload injury, linked to the growth-related changes in tissue composition and mechanical properties that precede skeletal maturity.

During the pubertal growth spurt, long bone lengthening temporarily increases the passive tensile load on traction apophyses via the associated muscle-tendon units. Simultaneously, the apophyseal cartilage is undergoing active ossification and is structurally immature relative to surrounding bone and tendon. This combination — increased mechanical demand from bone growth, reduced structural integrity from active remodelling — defines the vulnerability window.

Maturity-related variables (chronological age, skeletal age, pubertal stage, relative age effect within sport) are therefore included in LEAP-PHEN as covariates and potential effect modifiers, not merely as descriptive demographics. Their role as predictors of biomarker profile and treatment response will be examined in exploratory analyses.

Context, diagnosis, and clinical interpretation

The LEAP framework explicitly incorporates contextual and diagnostic factors as a fourth domain alongside tissue state, maturation, and mechanical demand. This recognises that clinical presentation, management decisions, and outcomes are shaped not only by the underlying pathology but also by:

  • Terminological inconsistency — "Osgood–Schlatter disease," "Osgood–Schlatter syndrome," "tibial tuberosity apophysitis," and other terms are used interchangeably in the literature, creating case-definition heterogeneity that limits evidence synthesis and comparability across studies.
  • Clinician and coach beliefs — qualitative data from the LEAP mixed-methods preregistration (OSF: KESA7) reveal that pathologising language, passive management recommendations, and poor communication about prognosis contribute to unnecessary worry, reduced self-efficacy, and disengagement from care.
  • Patient and parent experience — the lived experience of LEAP extends well beyond pain intensity: it includes perceived identity threat from sport withdrawal, frustration with diagnostic uncertainty, and the social cost of being unable to participate with peers.
  • Measurement tool availability — the absence of validated Polish-language patient-reported outcome measures (PROMs) for knee and foot/ankle in paediatric populations has been a practical barrier to standardised outcome assessment. The cross-cultural adaptation and reliability study (OSF: KESA7) addresses this directly for KOOS-Child and OxAFQ-C.
